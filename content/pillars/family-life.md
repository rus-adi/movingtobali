## Quick start (the fast version)

- If you’re brand new to the idea, **sketch your timeline** (next 3 months vs next year).
- Shortlist **2–3 areas** before you overthink school or housing.
- Pick a “calm baseline” routine: **transport plan + grocery loop + one weekly child anchor**.
- Keep **official links** bookmarked for anything that changes fast (especially visas).
- Prefer decisions that reduce stress: **short test-stays, staged commitments, clear contracts**.

If you want a copy/paste checklist for your first month, use: [First month setup checklist](/resources/first-month-setup-checklist).

## What family life usually feels like (once you’re past holiday mode)

Many families arrive in “holiday mode”—and then reality hits:
- commutes take longer than expected,
- groceries and routines take effort,
- kids need friends and structure.

The move starts feeling calm when you have:
- one predictable weekly rhythm,
- one community anchor,
- and a realistic commute.

## The calm roadmap

### Step 1: Decide your timeline

Write down the earliest date you could arrive—and the latest date you’d still feel good about.

### Step 2: Shortlist areas

Use the [Areas](/areas) hub to shortlist 2–3 places that match your family’s pace and commute tolerance.

### Step 3: Plan visas from experience (not certainty)

We share what families experienced and the questions to ask. We don’t guarantee outcomes. For changing details, use [Official links](/official-links).

### Step 4: Housing (staged, not rushed)

If possible: short stay → view in person → verify → sign. Rushing deposits is where families get burned.

### Step 5: Learning & community

A good-fit learning environment plus one parent community usually does more for “settling in” than any single logistics hack.

If you want the “community” piece to be easier, build it deliberately:
- choose one routine where you see the same parents repeatedly,
- show up consistently,
- and let relationships form over time.

## Community: how families actually meet people

Most families don’t “find community” by hunting for the perfect WhatsApp group.

In practice, community usually happens through:
- **school pick-up** (or camp pick-up),
- one weekly class/sport,
- a consistent market/cafe routine,
- volunteering (when it fits).

If you want a deeper guide: [Finding community in Bali with kids](/guides/finding-community-in-bali-with-kids).

## Culture with kids (respectful ways to join)

Culture is one of the best parts of living in Bali—especially for children.

Our stance is simple:
- participate respectfully,
- ask locals what’s appropriate,
- don’t treat ceremonies as performances.

Some families notice their kids become more independent here because daily life includes real-world practice:
